I first read *The Pragmatic Programmer* as a junior engineer and found it abstract. Reading it again with a few years of production scars, it hit differently.

## What Holds Up

**"Don't repeat yourself"** is obvious now because this book made it obvious. Same with the broken windows theory applied to codebases: small signs of neglect compound. I've seen this play out in every team I've been on.

The chapter on tracer bullets — building a thin end-to-end slice before filling in the full implementation — is still the best framing I've found for how to start a project without getting lost in architecture upfront.

## What Hasn't Aged Well

Some of the tooling advice is predictably dated. And a few sections feel like they're solving problems that modern frameworks have largely abstracted away.

The tone can also tip into preachiness. The "pragmatic philosopher" framing sometimes reads as self-congratulatory.

## The Core Insight

Strip everything away and the book is really about **taking responsibility** — for your code, your learning, your career. That part hasn't aged at all.

**Rating: 4/5** — Worth re-reading every few years.
